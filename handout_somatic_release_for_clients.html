Understanding Your Body
During a Session

What to expect, what is normal, and how to work with what arises rather than against it

The body speaks in sensations. Learning to listen without catastrophizing is one of the most important skills in healing work.

The Core Question
🤔
Is This Happening to Me or Moving Through Me?
The Framework
In a medicine session or a deep somatic healing experience, physical sensations can be intense. Heart pounding. Waves of nausea. Gut cramping. Pressure in the chest. Trembling. Heat. These sensations are real. They are genuinely occurring in the body. The question is not whether they are real, it is what they are.

Somatic emotional release and medical emergencies can feel similar from the inside. The difference lies in the quality, direction, and context of what is happening. A sensation that is moving, building and releasing, responding to breath and attention, arriving with emotional content, and resolving into relief is almost always emotional material completing itself through the body. A sensation that is escalating without release, localizing and intensifying without emotional context, and that does not respond to breath or grounding is worth telling your guide about immediately.

Most of what arises during healing sessions is the first kind. The body stores an enormous amount of unprocessed experience and it uses exactly these channels, the gut, the chest, the throat, the nervous system, to move that material through when the conditions are finally right. Understanding this does not mean ignoring symptoms. It means knowing how to listen to them.
The most important real-time tool is this question: is this getting better when I breathe into it and stay with it, or is it getting worse? Somatic release almost always softens with breath and attention. If something is escalating rather than waving, tell your guide.
Why the Body Does This
🫁
The Body Stores What the Mind Could Not Process
Somatic Science
When an experience is too overwhelming to be fully processed at the time it occurs, the nervous system routes the unfinished emotional material into the body. It does not disappear. It is held in the tissues, the gut, the chest, the throat, the muscles, waiting for a moment when conditions are safe enough to complete the processing that was interrupted.

The gut is particularly significant. The enteric nervous system, sometimes called the second brain, contains more neurons than the spinal cord and is in constant communication with the brain. The gut holds memory, emotion, and survival-level responses that the thinking brain has no access to. Nausea, cramping, gurgling, burping, and purging during healing sessions are very often the gut releasing exactly this kind of stored material.

Ayahuasca traditions have understood this for thousands of years. The purge is considered sacred, not a side effect to be managed. What comes up and out during a ceremony is the body completing something it has been holding. Many people report that physical purging is accompanied by a profound sense of release, relief, and emotional clarity that arrives precisely as the physical symptoms resolve.
The gut releasing during a medicine session is not a medical emergency. It is the medicine working. Nausea that builds, peaks, and resolves, often accompanied by burping, yawning, or tears, is almost always somatic release completing itself.
💓
The Heart and Chest in Healing Sessions
Common Sensations
Heart pounding, chest tightness, pressure, and intense activation in the chest are among the most common and most startling sensations that arise during medicine sessions. For anyone with health awareness, these can immediately trigger concern.

In the context of a healing session with appropriate screening, these sensations are almost always one of three things. They are the normal physiological effect of the medicine itself, as most psychedelics and empathogens produce temporary increases in heart rate and blood pressure. They are the body's activation as deep emotional material surfaces, the same racing heart that accompanies any intense emotion. Or they are the heart center opening, a felt sense of expansion, vulnerability, or grief moving through the chest that is consistently reported in healing work.

The question is direction. A heart that is racing and then gradually settling as the experience moves through is somatic release. A heart that is racing and accelerating, accompanied by chest pain radiating to the arm or jaw, shortness of breath that is not resolving, and a felt sense that something is physically wrong rather than emotionally intense, is worth telling your guide about right away.
All participants in healing sessions have cardiac screening before working with medicines that carry cardiovascular load. When that screening is clear, a pounding heart in session is almost always emotional activation, not a cardiac event. Tell your guide what you are feeling and let them assess.

Purging in Healing Sessions
🌊
What Purging Actually Means
The Body Completing
Purging in a healing session, whether it is vomiting, burping, yawning, crying, laughing, shaking, or sweating, is the body completing an arc that has been building, sometimes for decades. It is not a sign that something went wrong. In most traditions that work with plant medicines, purging is understood to be the most direct route to release.

Burping and yawning during a session are among the most commonly misunderstood forms of purging. They are not incidental. They are the vagus nerve discharging, the parasympathetic system completing a cycle of activation and release. They often arrive at precisely the moment an emotional wave resolves.

Crying is purging. Shaking and trembling are purging. Spontaneous sounds and vocalizations are purging. Deep gut nausea that moves upward through the system, producing burping, yawning, or eventual vomiting, that then resolves into clarity and relief, is one of the most reliable signatures of emotional material completing itself through the body. It is the medicine doing exactly what it is supposed to do.
Your guide will have an emesis basin available without any drama attached to it. You will be told before your session that nausea may arise and that it moving through the body is the healing working, not something going wrong. Let it come. Let it go.
What to Expect with Each Medicine
💉
Ketamine
Expected Physical Profile
Ketamine produces a reliable increase in heart rate and blood pressure, typically peaking in the first 15 to 30 minutes and returning toward baseline as the experience deepens. Nausea is common, particularly with higher doses. Dissociative sensations including feeling separate from the body, floating, and altered proprioception are part of the intended experience and are not causes for concern.

If nausea escalates without any wave quality or does not settle with repositioning, tell your guide. If you feel unable to breathe or experience sudden distress that does not respond to grounding, tell your guide immediately.
🍄
Psilocybin and Classic Psychedelics
Expected Physical Profile
Psilocybin and other serotonergic psychedelics produce modest increases in heart rate and blood pressure, pupil dilation, and sometimes jaw tension. Nausea on the come-up, in the first 30 to 60 minutes, is common. Yawning, sighing, and spontaneous emotional release are characteristic of the experience. Body temperature fluctuation, moving between hot and cold, is normal.

Tell your guide if you notice muscle rigidity, severe escalating agitation, or a rapid rise in body temperature that feels different from normal session warmth. These are rare but worth flagging immediately.
💊
MDMA
Expected Physical Profile
MDMA produces the most significant cardiovascular activation of the commonly used healing medicines. Elevated heart rate and blood pressure, sweating, jaw tension, and feeling very warm are all expected. These are not emergencies in a screened participant. They are the expected physiological signature of the medicine.

Tell your guide immediately if you feel extremely overheated and the warmth is not settling, if you feel confused or have a severe headache alongside nausea, or if your heart rate feels like it is continuing to accelerate rather than stabilizing.
Your Guide Has You
🛡️
You Are in Trained Hands
How your guide responds to what they observe teaches you how to relate to your own experience. If they remain calm and steady, your nervous system receives the signal that this is navigable, and it is. Your guide is trained to assess what is happening while remaining fully present with you, not watching from a clinical distance but sitting with you in real time.

During intense moments they will offer grounding support. A hand on the back if you have consented to touch, a steady voice reminding you to breathe, and words that normalize what is happening. "This is your body doing its work. You are safe. Breathe into it. Let it move." Your guide's calm, unfrightened presence in those moments is itself a form of medicine.

Your guide has done extensive somatic work of their own and has moved through their own intense experiences in healing contexts. This means they can read what is happening in the room with both clinical knowledge and lived understanding. They have been where you are. They will not be frightened by what frightens you.

Your guide has prepared thoroughly for this session. Emergency equipment is on hand including a blood pressure cuff, pulse oximeter, and AED. There is a clear emergency protocol in place and your guide knows exactly what steps to take if anything medically significant arises. You do not need to manage any of this. You do not need to monitor yourself or stay vigilant. That is your guide's job.

Your job is to be present with whatever arises and trust that you are held. Your only other job is to communicate any new symptoms or feelings to your guide as they arise. They cannot know what you are experiencing unless you tell them. Speaking what is happening, even in just a few words, gives your guide the information they need and keeps the two of you connected throughout the experience.
You do not need to diagnose what is happening. You do not need to decide whether something is serious. You need to feel it, breathe into it, and tell your guide what you notice. They will take it from there.
What to Do When It Gets Intense
🪑
In the Moment
For You
When physical sensations become intense during a session, the instinct is often to resist, to tighten, to try to make the sensation stop. This resistance is almost always what prolongs and intensifies the experience. The sensation is trying to complete. Resistance is what prevents completion.

What tends to help: slow the breath down and make the exhale longer than the inhale. This activates the parasympathetic nervous system and signals safety to the body. Bring attention directly into the sensation rather than away from it. Notice its quality, its location, whether it has a color or a shape or an emotion attached to it. Allow the body to make sounds, to move, to shake, to cry, to do whatever it is asking to do. These are the completion mechanisms the nervous system is trying to use.

Tell your guide what you are experiencing. Not because they need to fix it, but because speaking it aloud externalizes it slightly and makes it less overwhelming. Your guide's steady presence is a co-regulation resource. Use it.

If at any point you feel that something is physically wrong rather than emotionally intense, say so immediately. You are the only one who knows the quality of what you are feeling. Your guide needs that information.
It is almost never as medically serious as it feels in the moment. The medicine amplifies experience by design. Breathing into it and trusting the process is almost always the right move. Almost always.
✦ You Are Held
Your guide has trained extensively to recognize the difference between somatic emotional release and a genuine medical event. Most of what arises in a session is the body doing its work, the trembling, the nausea, the gut releasing, the chest opening, the grief moving through like a wave. Your guide has moved through their own versions of these experiences and will not be frightened by what frightens you. They know what the red flags look like, they have what they need on hand, and they are prepared to act if something genuinely medical occurs.

Your job is to trust the process. Their job is to hold the container. Both are in good hands.

When in doubt, breathe. When something feels physically wrong rather than emotionally intense, speak. Your guide is listening and they are ready.
