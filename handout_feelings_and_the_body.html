Feelings and the Body

Why we avoid emotions, what happens when we do, and how to metabolize them so they can finally set you free

Feelings are not the problem. Unfelt feelings are.

The Avoidance Trap
🚪
Why We Avoid Feelings
The Setup
Most of us were never taught how to be with our feelings. We were taught to manage them, push them down, stay busy, or stay strong. Over time, avoidance becomes automatic. It feels like survival. The trouble is that what we do not feel does not disappear.

It waits. In the body. In the nervous system. In the patterns we repeat. In the exhaustion we cannot explain. What began as a coping strategy becomes a cage, and the key is almost always feeling what we spent so long avoiding.
Avoidance is not weakness. It was the most intelligent adaptation available at the time. The cost of it, though, is always paid later.
When Avoidance Becomes Addiction
💊
Substances
Chemical Avoidance
Alcohol, cannabis, medications, and other substances that numb, soften, or create distance from emotional experience. These do not resolve the underlying feeling. They suspend it, often with interest added. The emotion that was too much to feel before is still waiting on the other side of the substance.
⚙️
Processes
Behavioral Avoidance
Overworking, scrolling, eating, exercising, gambling, shopping, or any behavior used compulsively to avoid sitting still with feeling. The behavior itself is not the problem. The compulsive use of it as an emotional exit is. For helpers, overworking is the most socially rewarded form of emotional avoidance.
🔗
People
Relational Avoidance
Using relationships, constant caretaking, or relentless connection to avoid being alone with your inner world. When another person's needs become a reliable escape from your own feelings, the relationship becomes a form of avoidance rather than genuine intimacy. Helpers are particularly vulnerable to this pattern.
All three paths work in the short term. That is why we keep using them. The cost is paid later, in the body, in the nervous system, in a life that could have been lived more fully.
Research-Backed Principle
"Name it to tame it."
When you can put a word to what you are feeling, your brain shifts from pure reactivity into understanding. Naming an emotion activates the prefrontal cortex and calms the threat response. The emotion does not disappear, but it becomes digestible. You do not have to solve the feeling. You just have to recognize it.

The simple act of saying "I notice I am feeling angry right now" changes the neurological relationship to that anger. This is not just mindfulness language. This is how the brain actually works.
The Fear of Feeling
😟
"If I Let Myself Feel It, I Won't Stop"
Most Common Fear
This is one of the most universal fears helpers carry. It makes sense. When emotions have been stored for years, they can feel enormous, bottomless, dangerous. The fear is not irrational.

Emotions are not permanent states. They are waves with a natural beginning, middle, and end. The average emotional wave, if not amplified by thought or suppressed by avoidance, peaks and passes within 90 seconds to a few minutes. It is the resistance, not the feeling itself, that makes the wave grow and persist.

The people who fear they will fall apart if they feel their grief are almost always the people who have been holding it the longest.
Emotions do not drown people who are willing to feel them. They drown people who have spent years running from them and are finally too exhausted to run.
🔥
Anger That Gets Stuck in the Body
An Example
Anger is designed to move through the body. It is activating energy, meant to signal a boundary violation and mobilize a response. When that response is suppressed, the activation has nowhere to go.

Anger without an outlet does not dissolve. It turns inward. It shows up as chronic muscle tension, headaches, numbness, cynicism, or sudden disproportionate explosions. The body holds what the mind refuses to acknowledge.

When anger is felt fully, expressed appropriately, and honored as information, it releases and reveals what it was always trying to say: this matters to me. Something here is not okay.
Why Expression Matters
😊
Imagine If You Were Never Allowed to Smile
An Analogy
Imagine that every time you felt happy, you forced yourself not to smile. You held back the laugh. You kept your face still and your voice flat. You felt the joy but you gave it nowhere to go.

It would not just feel strange. It would feel uncomfortable, and eventually painful. The emotion is there, but the body has no way to complete it. Joy without expression is joy held captive.

The same is true for anger, grief, fear, and shame. These emotions are not meant to live inside the body without expression. They are designed to move, to be felt, to come out in some form that allows the body to complete the cycle it started. When they cannot, they do not disappear. They wait. They wait for the chance to be known, for the moment when the person is finally willing or finally safe enough to let them through.

Expression does not have to be dramatic. It does not require a breakdown or an outburst. It simply requires that the feeling be allowed to exist in the body, that it be given a shape. A word. A breath. A sound. A movement. A tear. Any of these tells the nervous system that the emotion has been received, and that it is safe to release.
Feelings that wait for the chance to be known do not grow weaker over time. They grow more insistent. Healing happens when we stop making them wait.
How the Nervous System Responds to Emotion
When emotion arises, the nervous system responds in predictable ways. None of these responses are wrong. They are all protective adaptations. The goal is not to eliminate them but to understand them.
🌿
Free State
Regulated, present, and able to feel without being overwhelmed. Emotions flow, inform, and pass.
🧱
Brace
Tightening and holding. The body goes rigid to protect. Breath becomes shallow.
🪨
Collapse
Shutting down or dissociating when emotion feels too large to bear.
⚡
Fight or Flight
Mobilizing energy outward through anger, panic, urgency, or the need to escape.
🤝
Attach
Reaching for another person to regulate what feels unmanageable alone.
❄️
Numb
Feelings go quiet or flat. A hazy disconnection from your own experience.
🌊
Flooding
Overwhelmed by too much feeling at once. Emotion spills out in ways that feel uncontrollable or all-consuming. The system is past its capacity to regulate.
Metabolizing Emotion
🌱
What It Means to Metabolize an Emotion
The Goal
Just as the body metabolizes food to extract nutrients, we are designed to metabolize our emotions. When we move through a feeling fully, we gain something from it. Grief deepens compassion. Anger reveals a boundary. Fear points to what matters. Sadness makes room for joy.

When we do not metabolize an emotion, we get no nutrients from it. The feeling gets stuck in the body, accumulating in the tissues and nervous system. Over time this looks like chronic tension, reactivity, burnout, numbness, and a growing sense that something important has been lost.
When We Metabolize
The emotion moves through, releases, and leaves behind wisdom, clarity, or relief. The body softens. The nervous system completes the cycle it started.
When We Do Not
The feeling gets stuck. No nutrients extracted. Over time it shows up as tension, reactivity, burnout, numbness, or compulsive behavior.
🫧
When Feelings Fragment from the Event
Dissociation
Sometimes a person can describe something devastating, a trauma, a loss, an experience that changed everything, and do it without feeling anything at all. They report it matter-of-factly. They move on to the next topic. Their voice stays flat and their body stays still. This is not strength. This is dissociation.

What has happened is this: at the moment the event occurred, the emotional experience was too overwhelming to be felt. So the nervous system did something intelligent. It separated the feeling from the memory. The event got stored. The feelings got stored somewhere else. Disconnected. Floating in their own encapsulated bubble, not felt, and not associated with the origin event.

This is why a person can know intellectually that something terrible happened to them and feel nothing about it. The knowing and the feeling are in different rooms. The grief and pain and anger are still there, still present in the body, they just have no address. They do not know where they belong. They cannot be metabolized because they are not connected to anything they can metabolize from.

Healing in this context is not about feeling more. It is about reconnecting. Bringing the feeling back to the event that created it, gently, in the presence of a skilled guide, at a pace the nervous system can tolerate. When the feeling finally meets the memory it belongs to, something profound unlocks. The grief can grieve. The anger can move. The pain can speak. For the first time, it can begin to release.
Your skilled guide tunes into the moment when the feeling and the event are still separated and gently brings attention back there. That moment of contact is where the healing lives.
🔬
Modalities That Reconnect Feelings to Events
How Healing Happens
Certain healing approaches are specifically designed to do this reunification work, to bring fragmented feelings back into contact with their origin so they can finally be metabolized.

Ketamine-Assisted Healing and Psychedelic-Assisted Healing are among the most powerful of these. By temporarily loosening the rigid defenses that keep feelings separated from memory, these medicines create a window in which the emotional and cognitive experiences can find each other again. Under proper support and integration, a person may feel the grief or fear of an event they have never consciously allowed themselves to feel. This is not re-traumatization. It is completion.

EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) works directly on the dual processing of memory and emotion, helping the brain associate the feeling with the event and move both through to resolution.

Somatic practices, including Somatic Experiencing and Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, track the body's response in real time, following the thread of felt sensation back to where the feeling has been stored and held in waiting.

All of these approaches share a common understanding: healing is not forgetting. Healing is finally being able to feel what you could not feel then, in a body that now knows it is safe enough to do so.
Integration after any of these modalities is essential. The reconnection that happens in a session needs to be tended to, supported, and made sense of in the days and weeks that follow. This is where the wisdom is extracted.

What Emotions Need to Metabolize
Four Conditions
Emotions move through most completely when four things happen:

1. Felt in the body. Not thought about. Actually felt. The physical sensation of the emotion wherever it lives in the chest, throat, belly, or limbs.

2. Expressed in some way. Named out loud, written, moved through the body, or spoken to another person.

3. Witnessed. Seen by yourself or another person without judgment, minimizing, or the urge to fix it. Simple witnessing is one of the most healing acts available.

4. Made sense of. Understood in context. Given meaning. Integrated into the larger story of who you are and what you have been through. Put a name on it. Name it to tame it.

When all four happen, something shifts. Not because the situation changed, but because you moved through it rather than around it.
You do not need to feel everything perfectly or all at once. The direction matters more than the destination. Each time you choose to feel rather than flee, something in the system learns that it is safe to do so.
The Backpack Analogy
You have been carrying more than you know.
Imagine you have been wearing a backpack your entire life. Every trauma, every unprocessed emotion, every unfelt feeling gets placed inside it. Some things are boulders. Some are rocks. Some are tiny grains of sand.

Over time, the backpack gets heavier. You adjust. You compensate. You get stronger just to keep moving. You stop noticing how much you are carrying because carrying it has become normal.

Then one day, something small happens. A grain of sand lands in the backpack. You fall apart. To everyone around you, it looks like an overreaction. To you, it feels like something is deeply wrong with you. Neither of those things is true. The grain of sand did not break you. The weight of everything already in the backpack did.

This is what trauma work is. This is what metabolizing emotions does. Every time you feel a feeling fully, express it, witness it, and make sense of it, you are reaching into the backpack and pulling something out. First the grains of sand. Then the rocks. Eventually the boulders.

The backpack does not empty overnight. It lightens over time. Things that used to level you start to feel manageable. Small stressors stop triggering enormous responses. You find yourself with more capacity, more patience, more ease. Not because life got easier, but because the load you are carrying has finally started to shrink.
✦ A Note for Helpers
You have spent years holding space for everyone else's feelings. You have been trained to stay composed, to manage your reactions, to keep going. You have learned to leave your feelings at the door.

Feelings do not wait outside. They come in anyway, and they find places to live in the body where no one is looking.

Your own feelings deserve the same space you give to others. They deserve to be felt, expressed, witnessed, and made sense of. Not because it is comfortable, but because it is the only way they actually move.

Healing is not about falling apart. It is about finally letting yourself move through what has been waiting. You are allowed to feel your own feelings. All of them.
