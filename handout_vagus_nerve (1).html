The Vagus Nerve

The wandering nerve that connects the brain to the body, and why its health matters for everything else

One nerve, wandering from the brainstem to the gut. Tending it is one of the most direct paths to a regulated nervous system.

What the Vagus Nerve Is
🧠
The Wandering Nerve
The Basics
The vagus nerve is the tenth cranial nerve and the longest one in the body. Its name comes from the Latin word for wandering, which is exactly what it does. It originates in the brainstem and travels down through the neck, into the chest, and through the diaphragm into the abdomen, branching along the way to reach nearly every major organ.

It is the primary nerve of the parasympathetic nervous system, the part of the nervous system responsible for rest, digestion, and recovery. Where the sympathetic nervous system mobilizes the body for action, the vagus nerve does the opposite. It slows the heart, supports digestion, and signals the body that it is safe to settle.

Most people have heard the term but few understand how far this single nerve actually reaches, or how much influence it has over both physical and emotional regulation.
When people talk about feeling calm in their body, regulated, grounded, settled, they are very often describing a state in which the vagus nerve is doing its job well.
The Pathway Through the Body
🗺️
Following the Nerve Downward
Anatomy
The vagus nerve exits the base of the skull as a pair, one on each side of the body, and travels down the neck alongside the carotid artery and jugular vein. In this section it controls the muscles involved in swallowing and the voice box, which is part of why vocal tone and breath are so closely tied to nervous system state.

Continuing into the chest, the nerve branches extensively to reach the heart and lungs. Here it directly regulates heart rate, helps manage airway diameter in the lungs, and plays an important role in the body's inflammatory response.

After passing through the diaphragm, the nerve forms two main trunks that spread through the abdomen. From here it reaches the stomach, the small intestine, and the upper portion of the large intestine, regulating digestive enzyme release, stomach acid, and the wave-like movements that move food through the gut.

The vagus nerve's territory ends around the area of the colon known as the splenic flexure. This is an important and often misunderstood detail. The pelvic organs, including the bladder, rectum, and reproductive organs, are not part of the vagus nerve's territory. They are governed by a separate parasympathetic supply arising from the sacral spinal cord, sometimes called the pelvic splanchnic nerves.
Key Functions
💓 Heart and Lungs
Regulates resting heart rate, helps manage blood pressure, and influences airway diameter and breathing patterns. A well-toned vagus nerve supports a heart rate that can shift smoothly between activity and rest.
🍽️ Digestion
Controls stomach contractions, the release of digestive enzymes and acid, and the coordinated wave-like movements that move food through the gut. Many digestive complaints improve when vagal tone improves.
🛡️ Immune Response
Helps regulate the body's inflammatory response through what researchers call the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway. Chronic low vagal tone is associated with higher baseline inflammation throughout the body.
🗣️ Swallowing and Voice
Governs the muscles of the throat and voice box, affecting swallowing, vocal tone, and even sensation in part of the outer ear. This is why humming, gargling, and singing are used as vagal stimulation techniques.
The Sympathetic Nervous System
⚡
The Gas Pedal the Vagus Nerve Balances
Context
To understand why vagal tone matters so much, it helps to understand the system the vagus nerve is constantly working against. The sympathetic nervous system is the part of the autonomic nervous system responsible for the fight or flight response. It activates automatically in the presence of stress or perceived danger, preparing the body to react quickly.

When the sympathetic system activates, it increases heart rate, dilates the airways to bring in more oxygen, dilates the pupils, redirects blood flow toward the muscles, and slows or pauses functions the body does not need in an emergency, including digestion and bladder activity. The adrenal glands release stress hormones, primarily adrenaline and noradrenaline, which sustain this activated state throughout the body.

This system is essential for survival. In a genuine emergency, it can save your life. The problem is not that the sympathetic system exists. The problem is what happens when it activates too often, stays on too long, or fires in response to situations that are stressful but not actually dangerous, which describes a great deal of modern life and a great deal of helping work specifically.
Chronic sympathetic activation, sustained over months and years without adequate recovery, is associated with high blood pressure, cardiovascular strain, insomnia, chronic fatigue, and ongoing digestive problems. The body was designed for the sympathetic system to turn on and then turn back off. It was not designed to stay on indefinitely.
⚖️
Why This Is the Whole Reason the Vagus Nerve Matters
The Connection
The sympathetic nervous system is the accelerator. The vagus nerve, as the primary driver of the parasympathetic system, is the brake. A healthy nervous system needs both functioning well, the capacity to accelerate when something genuinely requires it, and just as importantly, the capacity to brake and return to baseline once the demand has passed.

This is the entire reason vagal tone deserves attention. A person can have a perfectly normal, even necessary, sympathetic response to a hard day, a difficult conversation, or a genuine crisis. What determines long term health is not whether the accelerator gets used. It is whether the brakes work. Strong vagal tone means the brakes work reliably. Weak vagal tone means the car keeps coasting in an activated state long after the foot has left the gas pedal.

For helpers specifically, the sympathetic system gets exercised constantly. Crisis response, vigilance, holding other people's distress, all of it activates the accelerator regularly and often. Without a strong vagal brake to match that level of activation, the nervous system simply stays revved. This is the physiological foundation of burnout, and it is exactly why strengthening the vagus nerve is not a wellness extra. It is the counterbalance that makes sustainable helping work possible.
What "Vagal Tone" Actually Means
A measure of how well your body can shift between activation and rest.
Vagal tone refers to the activity level of the vagus nerve and, more practically, to how efficiently the body can move out of a stress response and back into a calm, regulated state. High vagal tone means a nervous system that can become activated when needed and then settle again smoothly. Low vagal tone means a nervous system that stays activated longer than the situation requires, or that struggles to return to baseline at all.

Vagal tone is measurable and it is trainable. Heart rate variability, the natural variation in time between heartbeats, is one of the most common ways researchers and clinicians estimate vagal tone. Higher heart rate variability generally reflects higher vagal tone and a more flexible, resilient nervous system.
The Vagus Nerve and Mental Health
🌊
The Body's Brake Pedal
Polyvagal Connection
The vagus nerve plays a central role in how quickly and how fully a person recovers from stress and threat. When functioning well, it acts like a brake pedal, allowing the body to engage the sympathetic stress response when genuinely needed and then apply the brakes once the threat has passed.

When vagal tone is low, that brake pedal becomes weak or unreliable. The sympathetic nervous system, the fight, flight, or freeze system, ends up dominating more of the time, even in situations that do not call for it. This can look like chronic anxiety, a racing heart in non-threatening situations, difficulty calming down after conflict, digestive issues tied to stress, and a baseline sense of being on edge that never fully resolves.

This is also why so many somatic and trauma healing modalities focus directly on the vagus nerve. Polyvagal theory, developed by Stephen Porges, places the vagus nerve at the center of how humans move between states of safety, danger, and shutdown. Strengthening vagal tone is one of the most direct paths toward a nervous system that can hold difficulty without becoming overwhelmed by it.
The burping and yawning that often happen during somatic release in a healing session are, quite literally, the vagus nerve discharging. This is not incidental. It is one of the clearest physical signs that the parasympathetic system is completing a cycle of activation and release.
Signs of Low Vagal Tone
🔍
What to Notice
Self-Awareness
Low vagal tone can show up in many different ways across the body and the emotional landscape. A resting heart rate that runs higher than expected. Difficulty calming down after stress, conflict, or a difficult conversation. Digestive issues including bloating, constipation, or a stomach that reacts strongly to stress. A voice that feels weak or breath that feels shallow and high in the chest. Chronic low grade anxiety or a nervous system that feels permanently on alert. Trouble feeling fully relaxed even in objectively safe situations. Difficulty with deep, slow breathing without it feeling effortful.

None of these signs alone is diagnostic of anything specific, and many have multiple possible causes. But taken together, and especially in someone who recognizes the pattern of a nervous system that struggles to come down from activation, they point toward vagal tone as one piece of the picture worth strengthening.

For Helpers Specifically
💛
A Chronically Activated Nervous System Is an Occupational Hazard
The Helpers Wound
Helpers spend their working hours in a state of vigilance. Reading rooms, anticipating needs, managing crises, holding other people's pain. Over years, this can quietly erode vagal tone, leaving a nervous system that struggles to fully come down even on a day off, even on vacation, even when nothing is actively wrong.

This is not a personal failing. It is the physiological cost of sustained caregiving without adequate recovery, and it is one of the most under-recognized contributors to helper burnout. A helper with strong vagal tone is not just calmer. They are more capable of being fully present with the people they serve, because their own nervous system is not perpetually bracing.

The same practices that strengthen vagal tone for anyone are especially important for helpers, and the same barriers that make integration and rest difficult for helpers apply here too. Tending to your own nervous system is not separate from your capacity to help others well. It is the foundation of it.
A regulated nervous system is contagious in the best way. The people you serve will register, even unconsciously, whether the nervous system across from them is steady. Strengthening your own vagal tone is, in a very real sense, part of the care you offer everyone else.
✦ A Note for Helpers
The vagus nerve is not an abstract anatomical fact. It is the living thread that connects how safe you feel to how well your body functions, from your heart to your gut to your ability to recover from a hard day.

You cannot think your way into a regulated nervous system. You have to practice your way there, through breath, through sound, through movement, through connection. Small, repeated, embodied practices, not grand gestures, are what change vagal tone over time.

The wandering nerve that holds your whole body together deserves some attention of its own. Start small. Start today.
