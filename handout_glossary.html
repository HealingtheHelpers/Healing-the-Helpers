Trauma Terms & Definitions
A quick reference guide for accurate language during trauma talks, intake conversations, and client education.

Words matter. Having the right language for what you are experiencing is the first step toward understanding it, and healing it.

Core Burnout & Compassion Terms
Burnout
A syndrome of emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and reduced personal accomplishment. A cumulative occupational phenomenon marked by emotional depletion, withdrawal, and reduced effectiveness, driven primarily by workload, lack of control, and institutional stress rather than direct trauma exposure.
Emotional Exhaustion
The depletion of emotional resources and psychological resilience resulting from sustained interpersonal demands and chronic stress. A core component of burnout characterized by feeling drained, fatigued, and unable to replenish emotional reserves.
Compassion Fatigue
The convergence of burnout and secondary traumatic stress resulting from prolonged exposure to others' suffering. Includes emotional exhaustion alongside the emotional and physical effects of indirect trauma exposure, leading to reduced empathy and difficulty engaging in work relationships.
Empathy Fatigue
Distinct from burnout, stems specifically from continuous emotional investment in care and ongoing exposure to others' suffering. Characterized by diminished empathic engagement, emotional exhaustion, and coping difficulties. The specific toll of caring work without adequate recovery.
Emotional Fatigue
A state of being mentally and emotionally worn down. Related to but distinct from burnout. Can occur even in work you love when emotional demands exceed your recovery capacity. The sense of running on fumes emotionally.
Compassion Satisfaction
The pleasure, fulfillment, and meaning derived from helping others. The positive counterpart to compassion fatigue. When present, even difficult work feels purposeful and rewarding. Essential for sustainable helping work.
Loss of Purpose
The erosion of meaning and sense of professional or personal significance. A state where work that previously felt aligned with values no longer provides a sense of direction or accomplishment.
Moral Injury
The psychological and spiritual wound that results from perpetrating, witnessing, or failing to prevent actions that violate one's core values and moral code. Common in healthcare and emergency services when systemic failures or ethical compromises force difficult choices.
Moral Exhaustion
The depletion and emotional toll that comes from repeatedly witnessing systemic violations of values and ethical principles, often without the power to change them. The cumulative effect of being embedded in systems that operate against one's principles.
Trauma-Related Terms
Trauma
An overwhelming experience, actual or perceived threat to life, physical integrity, or psychological safety, that exceeds the nervous system's capacity to process. Results in lasting changes to how the nervous system responds to threat. Not defined by the event itself, but by the person's response to it.
Secondary Traumatic Stress
Indirect exposure to trauma through engagement with trauma survivors, characterized by post-traumatic stress symptoms such as intrusive thoughts, hyperarousal, and avoidance. Reflects the cumulative effects of witnessing and absorbing others' traumatic experiences.
Vicarious Trauma
The indirect effect of prolonged exposure to others' trauma that impacts the helper's worldview, belief systems, and emotional safety. Helpers can internalize clients' traumatic material, leading to symptoms resembling PTSD.
Re-traumatization
The activation of previous traumatic responses when current experiences or stimuli trigger memories, sensations, or emotional states associated with past trauma. Occurs when present circumstances unconsciously mirror aspects of past traumatic events.
Acute Stress Disorder (ASD)
A trauma response occurring within 3 days to 1 month after a traumatic event. Similar to PTSD but shorter in duration. Symptoms include intrusive thoughts, avoidance, negative mood changes, and heightened arousal.
Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)
A condition that develops after exposure to a traumatic event, lasting more than 1 month. Characterized by intrusive memories, avoidance, negative mood changes, and alterations in arousal. Reflects the nervous system's ongoing protective response to perceived threat.
Complex PTSD (C-PTSD)
Trauma resulting from multiple, prolonged, or repeated traumatic events, often interpersonal in nature. Results in more pervasive symptoms than single-incident PTSD, including difficulties with emotion regulation, self-perception, relationships, and sense of safety.
Attachment Trauma
Trauma occurring in the context of early relationships, usually with caregivers. These wounds shape how we relate to others throughout life and often manifest as relationship patterns, difficulty trusting, fear of abandonment, or fear of commitment.
Childhood Trauma / ACEs
Adverse Childhood Experiences, traumatic events during childhood including abuse, neglect, household dysfunction, loss of a parent, or other significant stressors. ACEs are cumulative: the more experienced, the greater the impact on adult health, relationships, and nervous system sensitivity.
Dissociation & Emotional Disconnection
Dissociation
A protective mechanism where the mind disconnects from the body, emotions, memories, or surroundings. Ranges from mild (spacing out) to severe. Develops as a survival strategy during overwhelming stress or trauma, allowing the nervous system to protect itself by creating distance from overwhelming experiences.
Depersonalization
A dissociative experience where you feel disconnected from yourself, as if watching yourself from outside your body, or as if your emotions are behind glass. You may feel numb, detached, or as if your body isn't really yours. Common in burnout, anxiety, and trauma responses.
Derealization
A dissociative experience where the external world feels unreal, foggy, or dreamlike. People, places, and events may seem distant or two-dimensional, like watching a movie rather than living your life. Often occurs alongside depersonalization.
Emotional Detachment
A coping mechanism where you emotionally disconnect from others and sometimes from yourself. Different from healthy boundaries, with detachment, you cannot access empathic connection even when you want to. An automatic protective response to chronic stress, empathy fatigue, or trauma.
Emotional Numbing
The inability to feel or access emotions. Related to dissociation and emotional detachment. Common in burnout and trauma responses. Often described as feeling blank, flat, or like nothing matters anymore.
Related States & Conditions
Anhedonia
The clinical loss of pleasure, joy, and the capacity for satisfaction in activities and relationships that previously brought fulfillment. A symptom of depression and trauma, characterized by emotional numbness and disconnection from what once mattered.
Languishing
A state of stagnation and emptiness where motivation, hope, and engagement are absent. Not clinical depression, but a middle ground of emotional depletion where things feel meaningless and the future feels uncertain.
Physical Health Decline
The somatic manifestation of chronic stress, trauma, and burnout expressed through deteriorating physical health, including increased illness, pain, immune dysfunction, and exacerbation of existing conditions.
Nervous System Terms
Hyperarousal / Hypervigilance
A state of elevated nervous system activation where the person is constantly scanning for threat. The nervous system is stuck in on-alert mode. Results in difficulty relaxing, sleeping, concentrating, and a heightened startle response.
Hypoarousal / Shutdown
A state of low nervous system activation characterized by numbness, disconnection, fatigue, and emotional flatness. The nervous system has overwhelmed itself and gone offline as a protective mechanism. Often mistaken for depression.
Nervous System Dysregulation
A state where the nervous system cannot maintain homeostasis, it shifts between hyperarousal (too activated) and hypoarousal (too shut down), often rapidly. Common in complex trauma and chronic stress.
Nervous System Regulation
The nervous system's ability to maintain balance between activation and rest. When regulated, the person can respond flexibly to stressors rather than being overwhelmed or shutting down. Achieved through somatic practices, secure relationships, and nervous system retraining.
Integration & Recovery Terms
Integration
The process of bringing fragmented parts, memories, emotions, sensations, back together into a coherent whole. Critical after breakthrough experiences such as healing sessions or EMDR. The neuroplastic window after a breakthrough is crucial for integration work.
Somatic (Body-Based) Processing
Trauma healing that works through the body rather than only through talk. Recognizes that trauma is stored in the nervous system and tissue, not just the mind. Includes yoga, breathwork, movement, massage, and body-centered practices.
Reclamation
The active process of claiming back one's agency, values, purpose, and vitality. An empowered orientation that emphasizes the individual's capacity to consciously rebuild their life and sense of self after depletion or trauma.
Yearning
The active desire and reach toward restoration of aliveness, purpose, and capacity. An acknowledgment of both the loss and the fundamental human longing to reclaim vitality and meaning.
Trauma Response & Nervous System Activation
Fight / Flight / Freeze / Fawn
The four primary survival responses of the autonomic nervous system when threat is perceived. Fight, mobilizing to confront the threat. Flight, mobilizing to escape. Freeze, immobilizing, playing dead, dissociating. Fawn, appeasing, people-pleasing, and complying to avoid harm. Fawn is especially common in helpers and those with childhood relational trauma.
Window of Tolerance
The optimal zone of nervous system activation, not too activated (hyperarousal) and not too shut down (hypoarousal), in which a person can function, feel, and process effectively. Trauma narrows the window. Healing widens it. Developed by Dr. Daniel Siegel.
Polyvagal Theory
Dr. Stephen Porges' framework describing three levels of the autonomic nervous system: Ventral Vagal (safe, social, connected), Sympathetic (fight/flight, mobilized), and Dorsal Vagal (freeze, shutdown, collapse). Understanding which state the nervous system is in at any moment is foundational to trauma-informed care.
Nervous System Activation
The process by which the autonomic nervous system responds to perceived threat or safety. Activation is not inherently bad, it is the nervous system doing its job. The problem arises when activation becomes chronic, stuck, or disproportionate to actual threat.
Triggers
Sensory, emotional, or situational cues that activate the nervous system's trauma response, not because present danger exists, but because something in the current experience resembles a past threat. Triggers are stored in implicit (body) memory, which is why they can feel immediate and overwhelming even when the conscious mind knows there is no current danger.
Startle Response
An exaggerated reaction to sudden stimuli, a loud sound, an unexpected touch, a sudden movement, common in PTSD, hypervigilance, and nervous system dysregulation. The startle response is mediated by the amygdala and reflects a nervous system on high alert.
Trauma Response
The automatic, involuntary reaction of the nervous system to a perceived threat. Trauma responses are not choices, they are the body doing exactly what it evolved to do. Shame about trauma responses is itself a wound that requires healing.
Regulation & Relational Terms
Co-regulation
The process by which one nervous system helps regulate another through safe, attuned presence, tone of voice, eye contact, and physical proximity. Co-regulation is the foundation of all early development, we learn to self-regulate only after experiencing co-regulation with a safe caregiver. It is also why therapeutic presence is so powerful.
Self-Regulation
The capacity to manage one's own nervous system activation, returning to the window of tolerance after being triggered or overwhelmed. Self-regulation is learned, not innate. It develops through co-regulation with safe others and can be rebuilt at any age through somatic, mindfulness, and relational practices.
Attunement
The capacity to accurately sense and respond to another person's emotional and physiological state. Attunement is the core of secure attachment, empathic care, and effective therapeutic presence. Chronic misattunement in early relationships is a form of relational trauma.
Rupture and Repair
The natural cycle in healthy relationships of disconnection (rupture) followed by reconnection (repair). Research shows it is not the absence of rupture but the presence of repair that builds secure attachment and resilience. Many trauma survivors never experienced adequate repair, and healing involves learning that reconnection after disconnection is possible.
Types of Trauma, Extended
Developmental Trauma
Trauma that occurs during critical periods of development, particularly in childhood, that disrupts the formation of secure attachment, healthy self-concept, and nervous system regulation. Developmental trauma shapes the fundamental architecture of how a person relates to themselves, others, and the world.
Intergenerational / Ancestral Trauma
Trauma that is transmitted across generations, biologically through epigenetic changes, relationally through parenting patterns, and culturally through story and silence. Research documents measurable physiological changes in the children and grandchildren of trauma survivors. What was not processed by one generation is carried by the next.
Collective Trauma
Trauma experienced by a group, community, or society, through events such as pandemics, natural disasters, war, systemic oppression, or mass violence. Collective trauma affects not just individuals but the social fabric of trust, safety, and shared meaning that holds communities together.
Medical Trauma
Trauma resulting from medical procedures, diagnoses, hospitalizations, or healthcare experiences in which the person felt fear, loss of control, pain, or violation. Medical trauma is significantly underrecognized, including among medical providers themselves, who may have their own unprocessed trauma from training and clinical experience.
Betrayal Trauma
Trauma that occurs when someone we depend on or trust violates that trust, a caregiver, institution, or system. The person must often suppress awareness of the betrayal in order to maintain the relationship necessary for survival. Common in childhood abuse, institutional harm, and systemic injustice.
Trauma Bonding
A strong emotional attachment that forms between a person and their abuser as a result of cycles of abuse and intermittent positive reinforcement. Trauma bonding is a nervous system response, not a character flaw, it is the predictable outcome of specific relational dynamics and explains why leaving harmful relationships is so difficult.
Occupational Trauma
Trauma accumulated through the cumulative experiences of a particular role or profession. For helpers, occupational trauma includes repeated exposure to death and dying, crisis, human suffering, systemic failures, ethical violations, and the cost of sustained caregiving without adequate support.
The Helper & The Wound
The Helper Wound
The often-unexamined personal wound that drives a person toward helping professions, a childhood experience of being needed, of earning love through service, of finding safety in caretaking others. The helper wound is not a disqualification, it often makes for the most gifted, empathic practitioners. But unexamined, it can lead to overextension, resentment, boundary collapse, and burnout.
Caretaker Role
A relational pattern, often developed in childhood, in which a person assumes responsibility for the emotional wellbeing of others, frequently at the expense of their own needs. When the caretaker role becomes a core identity rather than a chosen behavior, it can lead to chronic self-neglect and vulnerability to compassion fatigue.
People-Pleasing / Fawning
A trauma response rooted in early experiences where compliance, appeasement, and self-erasure were necessary for safety or connection. In adult helpers, people-pleasing often appears as inability to set limits, difficulty saying no, over-functioning, and placing others' needs consistently above one's own.
Hypervigilance in Helpers
The extension of the helper's threat-detection system into their professional role, constantly scanning for who needs what, anticipating others' needs before they are expressed, being unable to rest because someone might need something. A pattern that is rewarded in healthcare and emergency settings and deeply costly over time.
Healing & Post-Traumatic Growth
Post-Traumatic Growth
Positive psychological change that can emerge from the struggle with highly challenging life circumstances. Not the absence of distress, but the presence of new meaning, strength, connection, and appreciation that can coexist with it. Post-traumatic growth is possible, and it is what trauma-informed healing is designed to support.
Resilience
The capacity to adapt, recover, and grow in the face of adversity. Resilience is not a fixed trait, it is a dynamic set of skills, relationships, and resources that can be cultivated. It is not the absence of being affected by trauma, but the ability to move through it without being permanently defined by it.
Self-Compassion
The practice of treating oneself with the same warmth, understanding, and care one would offer a good friend. Research by Dr. Kristin Neff identifies three components: self-kindness (vs. self-judgment), common humanity (vs. isolation), and mindfulness (vs. over-identification). Self-compassion is one of the strongest predictors of resilience and recovery from burnout.
Neuroplasticity
The brain's lifelong capacity to form new neural connections, reorganize existing ones, and adapt in response to experience. Neuroplasticity is the biological basis of healing, it means that patterns formed in trauma are not permanent. Ketamine, EMDR, somatic practices, and mindfulness all work by activating neuroplastic change.
Somatic Experiencing (SE)
A body-based trauma healing approach developed by Dr. Peter Levine that works by gently completing interrupted survival responses stored in the nervous system. SE helps the body discharge held activation through titrated awareness of sensation, allowing the nervous system to return to regulation without re-traumatization.
EMDR
Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing, a trauma treatment developed by Dr. Francine Shapiro that uses bilateral stimulation to help the brain reprocess traumatic memories that are stuck in their original sensory intensity. EMDR has the most robust evidence base of any trauma treatment, with Level A evidence for PTSD from the WHO, APA, and Department of Veterans Affairs.
Trauma-Informed Care
An approach to care, practice, and organizational culture that recognizes the widespread impact of trauma, integrates knowledge about trauma into policies and practices, and actively seeks to avoid re-traumatization. The core principles are safety, trustworthiness, peer support, collaboration, empowerment, and cultural sensitivity.
Transpersonal & Spiritual Terms
Transpersonal Psychology
A field of psychology that incorporates ideas from various religious, cultural, and spiritual traditions alongside modern psychological theory to help individuals achieve personal growth, self-awareness, and well-being. Concerned especially with esoteric mental experience, mysticism, altered states of consciousness, and experiences beyond the usual limits of ego and personality.
Transpersonal
Extending or going beyond the personal or individual. Relating to psychology or healing concerned with experiences that transcend ordinary ego boundaries, including mystical states, peak experiences, altered states of consciousness, and encounters with what feels larger than the self.
Spiritual Bypassing
Using one's spirituality to avoid facing unresolved issues on a personal, interpersonal, or systemic level. Coined by psychologist John Welwood, the tendency to use spiritual ideas, practices, or frameworks to sidestep difficult emotions, trauma, relational wounds, or necessary accountability rather than moving through them.
Luminality
A neologism combining "luminous" and "liminal", referring to the transitional period in a ritual or healing process when participants temporarily abandon their normal roles and become immersed in the experience. During luminality, participants are neither here nor there, betwixt and between their pre- and post-ritual selves, existing in a threshold state of identity and possibility.
Gnosis
From the Greek word meaning knowledge or awareness. In spiritual and philosophical contexts, gnosis refers to direct, experiential, personal knowledge, not belief or theory but lived knowing. Distinct from intellectual understanding, gnosis is the knowledge that comes from direct encounter with truth, often arising in mystical states, medicine experiences, or moments of profound clarity.
Divination
The practice of seeking knowledge of the unknown, guidance, meaning, or foresight, through symbolic, intuitive, or spiritual means. Includes practices such as tarot, oracle, I Ching, runes, and dreamwork. In healing contexts, divination is used not as fortune-telling but as a tool for accessing intuitive wisdom and inner knowing through archetypal symbol systems.
Reciprocity
The symbiotic relationship between everything and everyone. A mutual relationship where giving and receiving are interconnected and interchangeable. From an indigenous perspective, it is what moves and sustains life through time and space in the microcosmos and the macrocosmos., Claudia Cuentas
Cognitive & Psychological Frameworks
Cognitive Dissonance
The psychological discomfort a person feels when their behavior does not align with their values or beliefs, or when they hold two contradictory beliefs simultaneously. The nervous system seeks resolution of the dissonance, which can drive either genuine change or rationalization to reduce the discomfort.
Experiential Dissonance
The contrast between how magical and alive an expanded or healing experience feels, and how flat, heavy, or small ordinary adult life feels by comparison afterward. Common after profound medicine sessions, spiritual experiences, or retreats. A normal and important part of integration that calls for gentleness rather than judgment.
Psychological Flexibility
The core capacity cultivated in Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), the ability to contact the present moment fully and change or persist in behavior in service of one's values. Comprised of six processes: acceptance, defusion, present moment awareness, self as context, committed action, and values. Strongly linked to resilience and recovery from chronic pain, trauma, and burnout.
Experiential Avoidance
The tendency to actively avoid or escape from uncomfortable internal experiences, emotions, thoughts, memories, or physical sensations. Considered a core mechanism of psychological suffering in ACT. Reducing experiential avoidance, learning to allow rather than escape what is uncomfortable, is a key pathway to healing.
Low Entropy State
A more rigid, structured, and predictable state of consciousness, associated with entrenched thought patterns, fixed self-concept, and habitual neural activity. The default waking state of most adults. In the context of psychedelic research, low entropy is associated with the ordinary ego-bound state.
High Entropy State
A less structured, more fluid, and unpredictable state of consciousness, associated with increased neural connectivity, dissolution of rigid patterns, and expanded perception. Psychedelics, deep meditation, and certain breathwork practices produce high entropy states. The neurological basis of why these experiences can reorganize fixed patterns of thought, feeling, and identity.
Brain Networks & Neuroscience
Default Mode Network (DMN)
A network of brain regions associated with mind-wandering, daydreaming, recalling memories, envisioning the future, and constructing the sense of self. Less active during focused tasks and more active during rest or introspection. In depression and trauma, the DMN can become overactive, producing chronic rumination, self-criticism, and stuck narratives. Psychedelics and meditation both reduce DMN activity.
Salience Network
The brain network responsible for identifying the most relevant stimuli among internal and external inputs, crucial for guiding behavior and allocating attention. Plays a key role in switching between the Default Mode Network (internal/self-focused) and the Executive Network (task-focused). Dysregulation of the salience network is implicated in PTSD and anxiety.
Executive Network
The brain network essential for maintaining and manipulating information in working memory, problem-solving, decision-making, and executing plans. Activated during tasks requiring focused attention and cognitive control. Chronic stress and trauma impair executive network function, contributing to difficulty concentrating, poor decision-making, and emotional dysregulation.
Heart Rate Variability (HRV)
A measure of the variation in intervals between heartbeats, reflecting the rhythmic oscillations of heart rate that occur with the breath. Higher HRV indicates greater capacity to handle stress and reflects healthy interaction between sympathetic and parasympathetic tone. One of the best available measures of nervous system resilience and a key indicator of recovery from burnout and trauma.
Somatic & Body-Based Terms, Extended
Titration
In somatic psychology, the process of gradually exposing a person to small, manageable amounts of distress related to a traumatic experience, allowing processing and release without overwhelm. Sometimes described as micro-dosing the trauma. Involves carefully monitoring physical sensations and adjusting the level of stimulation as needed, often paired with pendulation between the distressing experience and a safe, regulated state.
Pendulation
The somatic technique of moving back and forth between a state of activation or distress and a state of safety or regulation. Like a pendulum, the nervous system learns to move through difficulty rather than being stuck in it. Developed within Somatic Experiencing, pendulation prevents overwhelm while still allowing full processing of traumatic material.
Somatic Shame
The physical and emotional experience of shame as it lives in the body. Physical symptoms include chest tightness, shallow breathing, racing heart, stomach sickness, tension in the shoulders, and skin discomfort. Emotional symptoms include feeling small or unworthy, envy, rage, anxiety, fear of connection, and self-sabotage. Somatic shame must be addressed at the body level, not just cognitively, for lasting healing to occur.
Transmutation
The process of transforming difficult emotional or experiential material, pain, trauma, shame, grief, into something of value or meaning. In healing contexts, transmutation refers to the capacity to use the energy of suffering as fuel for growth, wisdom, and deeper aliveness. Reductions in experiential avoidance are considered a key mechanism of transmutation in acceptance-based approaches.
✦ A Living Document
Add terms as they arise in your talks or research. This glossary is designed to support clear, accurate communication about trauma and the helping professions, and to remind both helpers and those they serve that there is language for what they are experiencing. Being named is the beginning of being healed.
✦ A Note for Helpers
Language is a bridge. The more you understand the terrain of your own inner world, the more effectively you can navigate it. These words are tools, not diagnoses, not limits. Use them to build capacity for understanding yourself more fully.
