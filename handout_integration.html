The Work of Integration

What happens between the sessions is where the healing actually lives

The medicine opens the door. Integration is the act of walking through it.

What Integration Actually Is
🌀
More Than Processing What Happened
The Foundation
Integration is the process by which an experience becomes wisdom. It is what transforms a profound session into lasting change, a powerful insight into an actual shift in how you live, an emotional opening into a new way of being in relationship with yourself and others.

Without integration, even the most extraordinary healing experience fades. The nervous system returns to its defaults. The insight that felt like everything at 2am becomes a memory by the following week. Not because the experience was not real, but because the brain needs repetition, reflection, and embodied practice to wire new learning into lasting change.

Integration is not a single conversation or a single journaling session. It is an ongoing practice that begins the moment a session ends and continues for weeks, months, and in some cases years. The depth of the experience and the depth of the integration are what determine the depth of the change.
Many people underestimate how much integration matters because the medicine experience itself is so powerful. The session feels like the event. Integration is actually where the event becomes your life.
⏳
Why It Gets Skipped
The Common Pattern
Integration gets skipped for the same reason most important things get skipped: it does not feel urgent in the moment, it requires slowing down, and it asks you to stay with something uncomfortable rather than moving past it.

After a profound experience, two patterns tend to emerge. The first is that people feel so well they assume the work is done and return immediately to normal life, full schedule, full stimulation, no space to let what opened continue opening. The second is that the experience was difficult or disorienting and the impulse is to put it behind them and move on rather than moving through.

Both patterns close the integration window before it has done its work. The medicine or the experience creates a period of heightened neuroplasticity, a window in which the nervous system is more open to updating. That window does not stay open indefinitely. What you do in that window matters enormously.
Returning to alcohol, heavy social media use, overstimulation, or difficult relational conflict in the days after a session is one of the most common ways the integration window gets closed before it delivers its full value.
The Integration Window
The first 48 to 72 hours are the most important.
Research on neuroplasticity and healing practices suggests that the period immediately following a significant therapeutic experience represents a window of heightened openness to change. The nervous system is more permeable. New patterns are more likely to take root. Old defaults are temporarily less rigid.

This does not mean the integration work ends at 72 hours. It means the first 72 hours set the tone for everything that follows. What you protect this window from matters as much as what you bring into it. Treat it as sacred time. Your nervous system is in the middle of something important.
Physical and Somatic Integration
The body processes experience differently than the mind does. Somatic integration practices help the body complete what began in the session, and they are among the most important tools available.
🌿 Nature
Even a short walk outside, bare feet on grass or earth if possible, helps complete the grounding a session begins. Natural environments lower cortisol, regulate the nervous system, and provide sensory input that anchors presence in the body. You do not need a hike. You need outside.
🧘 Movement, Yoga, and Stretching
Gentle, intentional movement gives the body a language for what it is holding. Yoga, stretching, slow walks, and free movement all support the somatic processing that talk alone cannot reach. Follow what the body wants rather than a rigid routine.
🌬️ Breathwork
Intentional breath practice in the days following a session helps the body continue completing what the medicine started. Even 10 minutes of conscious breathing, box breathing, 4-7-8, or simply slow, full exhales, can support the nervous system in finishing its work.
😴 Rest and Sleep
The nervous system does profound processing work during sleep. More than most people realize. Prioritizing sleep in the days after a significant experience is one of the highest-value integration practices available. Protect your sleep. Reduce stimulation before bed. Let the body work.
🥗 Nourishing Food
The nervous system has done significant metabolic work. Whole, nourishing food, hydration, and reduced sugar and alcohol support the physiological recovery that integration requires. This is not about being perfect. It is about being intentional with what you feed a system that is working hard.
🐾 Animals
Time with animals offers uncomplicated co-regulation. Warmth, presence, and physical contact without any relational agenda. For nervous systems that are hypervigilant around human connection, animals provide safety that is immediately felt. A dog, a cat, a horse, time near any creature that does not require performance.
🤲 Bodywork and Touch
Massage, craniosacral therapy, or any skilled somatic bodywork can help release what the session moved but did not fully complete. Safe, intentional touch communicates to the nervous system what words cannot. If bodywork is accessible, the days following a session are an ideal time for it.
🛁 Warmth and Comfort
A long bath, a warm shower, soft clothing, a weighted blanket. Simple sensory comfort is not indulgence during integration. It is direct nervous system support. The body that has been in an open and vulnerable state needs signals that it is now safe and held.
Creative Integration
Creative expression bypasses the analytical mind and lets the experience find form without language. You do not need to be an artist. You need to make something with your hands.
📓 Journaling
Writing after a session allows the insights, images, and emotions that surfaced to find language before they fade. Do not try to make it coherent. Write whatever comes, fragments, contradictions, questions. The act of writing it down tells the nervous system this experience matters and is worth keeping.
🎨 Drawing and Coloring
Adult coloring, free drawing, sketching images from the experience, or anything that puts a mark on a page. The hands engaged in simple visual creation activate a part of the nervous system that analysis cannot reach. You do not need art supplies. A pen and paper is enough.
🎵 Music
Music chosen with intention can hold the emotional tone of what opened in a session and keep the integration space alive. Listen actively rather than as background. Let the music be the environment. Some people create playlists that carry the feeling of the session and return to them deliberately during integration.
🏺 Pottery and Clay
Working with clay or any tactile, malleable material is profoundly grounding and integrating. The hands in material that responds to pressure and shape mirrors the work of integration itself. If you have access to a pottery studio or even air-dry clay, this is worth exploring.
💃 Dance and Free Movement
Unstructured, expressive movement that follows the body's own impulses rather than executing technique. Ecstatic dance, authentic movement, or simply moving alone in a private space to music that holds the emotional field of the experience. The body knows how to integrate. Sometimes you just have to let it move.
✍️ Creative Writing
Poetry, free writing, letters to your younger self, letters to the parts of you that surfaced in the session. Creative writing gives the experience a form that holds it. It does not have to be good writing. It has to be honest writing.
Relational Integration
🗣️
Speaking It Out Loud
Community and Connection
Integration does not happen in isolation. The human nervous system is a social organ and it processes experience most fully in relational context. Speaking about what happened, to the right person, in the right way, is one of the most powerful integration practices available.

The right person matters. Not everyone in your life has the capacity to hold a profound or difficult healing experience without projecting their own reactions onto it. What you need in the integration window is someone who can listen without fixing, witness without minimizing, and stay present without making the experience about themselves.

This might be your guide, a therapist, a trusted friend who has done their own work, or a community of people who understand this kind of journey. What you are looking for is not advice. You are looking for witness.
Be discerning about who you share with in the early integration window. A dismissive or frightened response from someone you told too soon can close down the opening just as effectively as overstimulation can. Protect what is tender until it has had time to set.
🧑‍⚕️
Your Guide and Integration Support
Professional Support
A skilled guide does not disappear after the session ends. Integration support, whether through scheduled check-ins, integration sessions, or an ongoing therapeutic relationship, is part of what makes the work safe and effective.

If difficult material surfaced in a session, if the experience was disorienting or overwhelming, if you are noticing heightened emotion, strange sleep, or unusual sensitivity in the days following, reach out to your guide rather than waiting for the next scheduled contact. Integration can bring things to the surface that need skilled support. That is not a sign that something went wrong. It is often a sign that something important is moving.

If you are working with a therapist in addition to a medicine guide, the integration period is an especially valuable time to bring that therapist the material from the session. They can help you make sense of what arose and anchor it in your ongoing therapeutic work.
What to Protect the Space From
🛡️
Integration Is Also About What You Avoid
Protecting the Window
What you keep out of the integration window matters as much as what you bring into it. The nervous system in the days after a significant healing experience is genuinely more open and more permeable than usual, which means it is also more vulnerable to disruption.

Alcohol and cannabis (unless cannabis is being used intentionally as part of integration with guidance) dampen the neuroplasticity that makes integration possible and interrupt the sleep processing the nervous system depends on.

Social media and news flood the nervous system with stimulation and reactivity at the exact moment it most needs quiet and space. Even a few minutes of scrolling can pull the attention away from the inner work and replace it with external noise.

High-conflict conversations and difficult relational dynamics in the first few days can use up the nervous system resources that would otherwise go toward integration. This does not mean avoiding life, but it does mean being intentional about what you take on.

Overscheduling is one of the most common ways helpers specifically disrupt their own integration. The moment the session ends, the instinct is to get back to being useful, to return to the list, to fill the space. The space itself is the medicine during integration. Protect it.
You do not have to be perfect about any of this. One glass of wine or one evening of Netflix does not ruin an integration. The intention matters more than the compliance. Ask yourself before each choice: does this support what is happening in me right now, or does it interrupt it?
Integration Is Not a Week
📅
The Work Continues Long After the Experience Fades
The Long View
Many people expect integration to be complete within a week or two of a session. For experiences that touch deep material, the real integration often unfolds over months, and occasionally over years. This is not a sign that something is wrong. It is the nature of how humans change.

A profound healing experience can plant something that takes a long season to fully grow. You may not understand what a session was about until three months later, when a moment in ordinary life suddenly illuminates what was opened. You may find yourself returning to the same insight in progressively deeper forms, each time receiving it at a new level of the body.

Integration is complete not when you have analyzed the experience but when it has changed how you live. When the new story is not something you are working to believe but something you simply are. When the nervous system that once contracted in response to a familiar trigger now has a different option available. That kind of change takes the time it takes.
Signs that integration is working: you notice yourself responding differently in situations that used to be automatic. You feel more spacious around something that used to feel tight. You are less reactive, more resourced, more able to be with yourself without needing to escape. These are small signs and they are everything.
🔦
When to Seek More Support
Knowing the Signs
Most integration challenges are normal and resolve with time and practice. Some warrant additional support. Reach out to your guide or a mental health professional if you are experiencing any of the following in the weeks after a session.

Persistent difficulty functioning in daily life, at work, in relationships, or in basic self-care that does not improve after the first week or two. Intrusive thoughts or images from the session that feel unwanted and do not settle. Significant increase in anxiety, depression, or dissociation that feels different from your baseline. Difficulty distinguishing ordinary reality from the experience or thoughts that feel outside your own control. Impulses toward self-harm or harm to others.

These experiences are not common, but they are real, and they are not a sign of failure. They are a sign that the system opened something that needs skilled support to complete. Getting help is the integration practice in those moments.
For Helpers Specifically
💛
The Hardest Part for Helpers Is Allowing the Space
The Helpers Wound
Helpers are trained to be useful. To be in motion. To show up for others. The integration period asks the opposite. It asks you to be slow, inward, receptive, and temporarily unavailable in some of the ways you are usually available.

For someone whose sense of value is tied to their function, this can feel not just uncomfortable but wrong. The untended inbox, the colleague who needed something, the patient who called. The internal pressure to get back to being of service can be overwhelming in the exact window when the deepest integration work is happening.

Protecting your integration time is not selfishness. It is the work. The healing you are doing in this window is the same healing that will eventually make you a more present, more resourced, and more genuinely helpful clinician, parent, partner, and person. You cannot give from an empty place indefinitely. Integration is how you refill.

Tell the people closest to you that you need a few days of gentleness. Lower the expectations you place on yourself. Let the list wait. The most important thing happening right now is the thing that is happening inside you, and it deserves your full attention.
You have spent years building the capacity to hold space for other people's most tender and difficult moments. You deserve that same quality of holding for your own.
Integration at a Glance
First 48 to 72 hours: nature, gentle movement, nourishing food, rest, animals, limited screens and alcohol, protect the space.

First two weeks: journaling, creative expression, music, bodywork, intentional breathwork, one trusted conversation, check-in with your guide.

Ongoing: notice how you are changing in ordinary moments. Return to the insights that surfaced. Bring the material to your therapist. Let the integration continue doing its work even when the experience itself has faded.

Always: be gentle with yourself. The nervous system is working. You do not have to make it happen. You have to allow it to happen.
✦ A Note for Helpers
Integration is the part of the healing journey that gets the least attention and does the most work. It is not glamorous. There is no peak experience, no breakthrough moment, no dramatic shift. It is quiet, ongoing, and it asks more of you than the session itself did in some ways, because it asks you to remain with yourself in ordinary life and let what opened continue to change things.

You are worth that time. The healing is worth that time. What is being rewired in you matters not just to you but to everyone you will ever sit with, care for, or love.

Stay close to it. Let it land. Let it stay.
