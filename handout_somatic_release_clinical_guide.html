Somatic Release vs
Medical Emergency

Clinical reference for guides and providers — for internal use

The body speaks in sensations. Your clinical job is to listen, assess, and stay unfrightened.

The Core Framework
🧭
Quality, Direction, and Context
Clinical Assessment
In a medicine session or a deep somatic healing experience, physical sensations can be intense. Heart pounding. Waves of nausea. Gut cramping. Pressure in the chest. Trembling. Heat. These are real sensations occurring in the body. The clinical question is what they are, not whether they are real.

Somatic emotional release and medical emergencies can look similar from the outside and feel similar from the inside. The difference lies in the quality, direction, and context of what is happening. A sensation that is moving, building and releasing, responding to breath and attention, arriving with emotional content, and resolving into relief is almost always emotional material completing itself through the body. A sensation that is escalating without release, localizing and intensifying, arriving without emotional context, and that does not respond to grounding or breath is more likely to be a genuine medical event.

Most of what arises during healing sessions is the first kind. The body stores an enormous amount of unprocessed experience and uses the gut, the chest, the throat, and the nervous system to move that material through when conditions are finally right. The most important real-time clinical tool is the question: is this waving or escalating?
Somatic release almost always softens with breath and attention. Medical emergencies almost never do. Hold this as the primary triage heuristic throughout the session.

Purging
🌊
What Purging Means and How to Hold It
Clinical Notes
Purging in a healing session, whether it is vomiting, burping, yawning, crying, laughing, shaking, or sweating, is the body completing an arc that has been building, sometimes for decades. It is not a sign that something went wrong. It is the medicine working.

Burping and yawning are among the most commonly misunderstood forms of purging. They are not incidental. They are the vagus nerve discharging, the parasympathetic system completing a cycle of activation and release. Watch for these as positive clinical signs that the nervous system is completing its work.

Deep gut nausea that moves upward through the system, producing burping, yawning, or eventual vomiting, that then resolves into clarity and relief, is one of the most reliable signatures of emotional material completing itself through the body. Provide an emesis basin without drama. Normalize it in the preparation session before it happens. Your response to purging teaches the person how to relate to it. Stay calm, stay present, stay unfrightened.
Distinguish purging from a medical vomiting event by the trajectory. Purging in session builds, releases, and resolves into relief. Medical vomiting escalates, is accompanied by increasing distress, and does not produce the felt sense of release.
Medicine-Specific Physical Signatures
💉
Ketamine
Expected Profile and Red Flags
Ketamine produces a reliable increase in heart rate and blood pressure, typically peaking in the first 15 to 30 minutes and returning toward baseline as the experience deepens. Nausea is common, particularly with higher doses and certain routes. Dissociative sensations are part of the intended experience and are not medical concerns.

Clinical watch points: Nausea that escalates without any wave quality and does not respond to repositioning or grounding. Laryngospasm, rare but reported, presents as sudden inability to breathe. Emergence reactions, agitation and distress during the return phase, require supportive presence rather than intervention in most cases. Significant blood pressure elevation in people with uncontrolled hypertension warrants monitoring and possible early termination.
🍄
Psilocybin and Classic Psychedelics
Expected Profile and Red Flags
Psilocybin and other serotonergic psychedelics produce modest increases in heart rate and blood pressure, pupil dilation, and sometimes jaw tension. Nausea on the come-up is common. Yawning, sighing, spontaneous emotional release, and body temperature fluctuation are normal and expected.

Clinical watch points: Signs of serotonin syndrome if there is any question of undisclosed serotonergic medications, including rapid escalating hyperthermia, muscle rigidity, clonus, and severe agitation occurring together. Psychological crisis that is not moving or resolving and that is creating risk to the person or others. Any history of bipolar disorder or psychosis warrants heightened monitoring throughout.
💊
MDMA
Expected Profile and Red Flags
MDMA produces the most significant cardiovascular load of the commonly used healing medicines. Elevated heart rate and blood pressure, sweating, jaw tension, and feeling very warm are all expected in a screened, healthy participant.

Clinical watch points: Hyperthermia above 39.5 degrees C (103.1 F) requires active cooling and medical attention. In rare cases with undisclosed medications, serotonin syndrome. Hyponatremia if someone has consumed excessive water, presenting as confusion, headache, and nausea that is different in quality from session nausea. Heart rate that continues to accelerate without settling over the first hour warrants close monitoring and possible early termination.
⛔ Activate Emergency Protocol — These Are Not Somatic Release
Chest pain that radiates to the arm, jaw, neck, or back, especially with diaphoresis and pallor.

Temperature above 39.5 C / 103.1 F that is not responding to cooling measures, particularly with MDMA or in hot environments.

Seizure activity, especially in anyone with a history of seizures, lithium use, or stimulant combinations.

Loss of consciousness that is not simply dissociation and from which the person cannot be aroused.

Respiratory distress, labored breathing, cyanosis, or inability to breathe that does not resolve with positioning and presence.

Escalating agitation with physical danger to self or others that cannot be de-escalated with guide presence and environmental management.

Signs of serotonin syndrome together: hyperthermia, muscle rigidity, clonus, hyperreflexia, and severe agitation, particularly with any undisclosed medication history.

Severe hypertension with symptoms: headache, visual changes, and confusion in someone with known cardiovascular risk.

Your clinical gut says something is wrong. Clinical intuition is a legitimate diagnostic tool. If something feels medically wrong rather than emotionally intense, trust that.
Reading the Room and Guide Protocol
🧭
Clinical Presence in Real Time
For the Guide
The guide's job during intense somatic moments is to be the calm regulating presence that the person's nervous system is co-regulating with. How you respond to what you see teaches the person how to relate to their own experience. If you become alarmed, their alarm increases. If you remain steady and curious, their nervous system receives a signal that this is navigable.

When intense physical symptoms arise, the clinical assessment happens simultaneously with the relational presence. You are watching the person while sitting with them, not watching them from a clinical remove. Key questions to hold internally: is this escalating or waving? Is the person oriented and able to respond? Is there emotional content accompanying the physical sensation? Is there any change in color, temperature, or responsiveness that suggests a physiological shift beyond the expected medicine profile?

Routine somatic support during intense moments includes a hand on the back or shoulder if consented and appropriate, slow grounding verbal guidance, reminding the person to breathe, and normalizing what is happening. "This is your body doing its work. You are safe. Breathe into it. Let it move." These words, delivered from a steady and unfrightened presence, are a clinical intervention.
The guide who has done their own somatic work and who has moved through their own intense physical experiences in healing contexts will read the room more accurately than one who has not. Your own embodied experience of somatic release is part of your clinical training.
📋
Session Preparation Checklist
Before Every Session
The best management of a medical emergency in a session is prevention through thorough screening. Every participant should have completed a comprehensive medical history including cardiovascular history, current medications, seizure history, and psychiatric history before sitting with any medicine that carries physiological load.

Session space must have on hand: blood pressure cuff and pulse oximeter for baseline and spot checks, emesis basin, water and electrolytes, AED, a clear emergency protocol including the nearest emergency room and the fastest route, and at least one person who is not the primary guide and who is prepared to call emergency services if needed.

The primary guide should not be the one to call emergency services if that becomes necessary. They need to remain present with the person. Assign this role explicitly before the session begins.

Know your medicine-specific support options. For ketamine, benzodiazepines can reduce emergence reactions. For serotonin syndrome, cyproheptadine if available. For any cardiovascular emergency, basic life support certification should be current for all providers in this space.

Run a brief mental emergency drill before each session. Where is the nearest emergency room? What is the fastest exit? Who calls? Who stays? Thirty seconds of preparation protects everyone in the room.
Document baseline vitals before every session and spot-check at 30-minute intervals or whenever clinical concern arises. This documentation matters both for safety and for the clinical record.
✦ A Note for Guides and Providers
The ability to distinguish somatic emotional release from a genuine medical emergency is one of the most important clinical skills in this work, and it is one that develops with experience, embodied practice, and honest reflection on your own sessions.

Most of what you will see in a session is the body doing its work. The trembling, the nausea, the gut releasing, the chest opening, the grief that moves through like a wave and leaves someone lighter on the other side. Your job in those moments is to be the calm certainty that the person cannot access for themselves.

The rare medical event is real and worth preparing for. Know the red flags. Have the equipment, including the AED. Run the mental drill. Carry your CPR certification current. Know the nearest emergency room.

Then trust the body. Trust the medicine. Trust the process. Stay present, stay unfrightened, and let the healing happen.
