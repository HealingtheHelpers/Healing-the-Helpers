Understanding Transference

Why the healing relationship brings up old feelings, and how to work with them rather than be surprised by them

Transference is not a problem. It is the past arriving in the present, asking finally to be seen.

What Transference Is
🔄
The Past Showing Up in the Present Relationship
The Definition
Transference is what happens when feelings, expectations, and relational patterns from an earlier relationship, most often with a parent or primary caregiver, get unconsciously redirected onto a person in the present. You are in a relationship with your guide, but the feelings that arise are not only about your guide. Some of them belong to someone else, from somewhere else, from a long time ago.

The word was first used by Sigmund Freud to describe what happened between patients and therapists, but the phenomenon itself is universal and ancient. Wherever there is a relationship with a significant power differential, wherever one person holds care, authority, or influence over another, the nervous system reaches back into its relational archive and applies familiar patterns to the new situation.

Transference is not imagination and it is not a sign that something is wrong with you. It is the nervous system doing what it was designed to do: use past experience to navigate present relationships. The problem arises only when the pattern runs without our awareness, when we react to our guide as if they were our mother, our father, or the person who failed us most, and we do not know that is what is happening.
Transference is information. It points directly at the relational wounds that most need attention. When something about your guide or the healing relationship feels disproportionately intense, that disproportionality is worth getting curious about.
Why It Happens
🧠
The Nervous System Recognizes Patterns
The Mechanism
The nervous system learns from relational experience from the very beginning of life. Before language, before conscious memory, the body is learning: is this person safe? Will this person stay? Am I too much for this person? Will love be withdrawn if I express what I actually feel? These early lessons become templates, and when a new relationship activates similar conditions, the template fires.

A healing guide who is caring, attuned, and fully present with you may be the first adult who has held that quality of attention for you in a one-on-one relationship. That experience can activate the part of you that needed exactly this as a child and never received it. What comes up is not just gratitude toward the guide. It is grief, longing, attachment, and sometimes rage at everyone who was not this, flooding the present moment through the channel of the current relationship.

The nervous system does not timestamp its memories. It does not say "this feeling belongs to 1987 and this one belongs to now." It simply responds to resonance. A guide who feels safe may feel like the parent you always needed. A guide who sets a limit may feel like the one who abandoned you. Neither perception is entirely about the guide. Both are doorways into the original material.
This is also why healing relationships can feel more intense than ordinary ones. You are not just relating to the person in front of you. You are, in some sense, relating to everyone who came before them. That is a lot of weight for one relationship to carry, and understanding this is what makes it workable.
Forms Transference Takes
Transference does not always look the same. It can arrive as warmth and idealization, as hostility and suspicion, or as something that feels harder to name. All of these are the same underlying mechanism expressing itself in different directions.
Positive Transference
Feeling overwhelming warmth, love, or gratitude toward your guide that feels larger than the relationship has earned. Idealizing them. Wanting their approval intensely. Feeling unusually safe in their presence, as if they are the only person who truly understands you. Wanting to please them or dreading disappointing them.

This can feel wonderful and can support the healing work. It becomes worth examining when the feelings are so intense that they are difficult to bring into honest conversation, or when they persist in ways that pull you toward dependency.
Negative Transference
Feeling sudden distrust, irritation, or anger toward your guide that seems disproportionate to what actually happened. Interpreting neutral actions as disapproval or rejection. Feeling judged, dismissed, or unseen even when your guide is being careful and present. Wanting to withdraw or push them away.

This often arrives when the guide does something, sets a limit, misses a cue, says something imperfectly, that resonates with an old relational wound. The feeling is real. Its source may be older than this relationship.
Idealizing Transference
Seeing your guide as having something you lack, wisdom, peace, wholeness, and projecting onto them a kind of perfection. Wanting to be like them. Measuring yourself against them and feeling inadequate. This often involves placing the guide in the role of the idealized parent, the one who finally knows what to do, who will fix what is broken.

Idealization protects against the risk of disappointment and can be an early form of trust. It becomes limiting when it prevents honest engagement and blocks you from seeing your guide as a full, fallible human being.
Erotic Transference
Feelings of romantic attraction or intense personal longing toward your guide. This is more common than most people expect and far more common than most people admit. It is often a form of the longing for deep, safe, attuned connection being expressed in the language the nervous system has available, which sometimes codes intimacy and safety as romantic or erotic experience.

This is important to understand and important to bring to your guide or a separate therapist rather than act on. The feelings are real. Their source is usually something deeper than attraction to the specific person.
On Erotic Transference
The longing for deep connection sometimes speaks in the wrong language.
Erotic transference deserves its own attention because it is the form of transference most likely to be acted on, most likely to cause harm, and most likely to go undiscussed because both the person experiencing it and the guide they are experiencing it toward feel awkward naming it.

The longing underneath erotic transference is almost always the longing for attunement, for someone to know you completely and stay, for intimacy that does not require performance or self-editing. That longing is real and important and worth healing. The specific attraction to the guide is almost always the messenger, not the message.

A skilled guide maintains clear ethical boundaries regardless of what arises in the transference. This is not rejection. It is the most important thing a guide can do for the healing. The boundary itself, held with warmth and clarity, is often the corrective experience the transference was pointing toward all along.
Transference in Medicine Sessions
✨
The Medicine Amplifies Everything
Why This Matters in Healing Work
In an ordinary therapeutic relationship, transference emerges gradually over weeks and months as the relationship deepens. In a medicine session, it can emerge fully formed within minutes. The altered state reduces the defensive filtering that ordinarily modulates how much we allow ourselves to feel in a relational context, and the intensity of the experience creates conditions that are ripe for early attachment material to surface.

Your guide may become, in the space of a session, the most important person in the world. You may feel you love them in a way you have never loved anyone. You may feel rage at something small they did or did not do that seems, in the moment, catastrophic. You may feel abandoned if they move to the other side of the room. You may feel seen for the first time in your life, or exposed and judged, or both within the same hour.

All of this is normal. All of it is information. None of it is the final truth about your guide or about your relationship with them. It is the medicine opening the relational archive and letting the old material into the light. The guide's job in this is not to respond to the transference as if it is about them. It is to hold it steadily, name it when useful, and use it to help you go deeper toward what it is actually about.
The intensity of feeling in a medicine session is not evidence of the depth of the relationship with the guide. It is evidence of the depth of what you are carrying. A skilled guide knows this and will not be inflated by your love or wounded by your anger. They will stay curious about both.
🪞
What the Transference Is Actually About
Following It Back
The most useful question when you notice an intense or surprising feeling toward your guide is: who does this remind me of? Not in a detached, analytical way, but as a genuine inquiry. What is the earliest version of this feeling? When did I first feel this kind of longing, or this kind of fear, or this kind of need to be chosen?

The guide is almost always a stand-in. Not because they are interchangeable with the original person, but because the nervous system has assigned them a role in a much older drama. Following the feeling back to its origin is where the healing lives. The transference is a thread. Pulling it gently leads you toward the wound it is attached to.

This is one of the reasons that integration conversations with your guide after a session are so valuable. What felt like intense love or rage or longing during the session can, in the cooler light of the following days, be traced back to something specific. Something that can finally be seen, named, and worked with.
Counter-Transference
🔁
When the Guide Has Feelings Too
The Other Direction
Counter-transference is what happens when a guide or therapist has their own emotional reactions to the person they are working with, reactions that are shaped by the guide's own unprocessed history rather than by what is actually happening in the room. It is transference in the other direction.

A guide who had a critical parent may feel an urge to over-reassure and fix when someone in the chair expresses pain. A guide whose own needs were dismissed may struggle to hold firm limits with people who push against them. A guide who was a parentified child may unconsciously foster dependency in the people they work with. Counter-transference is not a sign that someone is a bad guide. It is a sign that they are human. The problem arises only when it is unexamined.

This is why guides who do their own ongoing therapeutic work, supervision, and personal healing are better guides. Not because they are free of counter-transference, but because they know their own patterns well enough to catch them before they contaminate the work. A guide who has not done their own work is far more likely to use the healing relationship, unconsciously, to get their own needs met.

You deserve a guide who is in their own process. Not one who has finished healing, but one who is actively engaged with it and who has the humility and the support structure to recognize when their own material is showing up.
If you notice your guide becoming unusually invested in your progress, uncomfortable with your anger toward them, or pulling for particular outcomes, these may be signs of counter-transference worth naming in a direct conversation or with a separate therapist.
Working With Transference
💬
Name It Before It Names You
The Practice
The most powerful thing you can do with transference is name it. In your journal, in an integration conversation, in a session with a separate therapist, or directly to your guide when the relationship has enough safety to hold it. "I notice I want your approval more than feels proportionate to how long we have known each other." "I felt a flash of anger when you said that and I am not sure it is actually about you." "I feel a kind of love for you that I think is probably about something older than this."

Naming it does several things at once. It externalizes the feeling enough to be able to look at it. It reduces the likelihood that you will act on it in ways that complicate the relationship. It opens a conversation that, when held well, goes directly into the heart of what the transference is pointing toward. And it gives the original wound a chance to be witnessed in the present, in a relational context that can offer something different than the original one did.

This is the corrective emotional experience that the transference is often seeking without knowing it. Not the enactment of the old dynamic, but its exposure to a response that is different. Kinder. More boundaried. More honest. More present. That difference, received and felt, is what begins to update the template.
You do not have to understand the transference completely before naming it. "This feels bigger than it should" is a perfectly complete observation to bring to a guide or therapist. They can help you follow it from there.
🛡️
Boundaries Are the Healing, Not the Obstacle to It
Ethics and Safety
One of the most important things to understand about transference is that the guide's maintenance of clear ethical limits is not a barrier to the healing. It is the healing. When a guide holds a firm, warm limit in the face of intense transference, they are demonstrating exactly what the wounded attachment system most needs to experience: that care does not require merger, that intimacy does not require the dissolution of appropriate limits, that someone can be fully present with you without becoming what the original relationship became.

A guide who allows the limits to erode, who responds to positive transference by getting closer, by accepting specialness, by allowing the relationship to become something other than what it is, is not giving the person what they need. They are re-enacting the original wound under a different name. The person needed a parent who could hold their love and their longing without crossing into inappropriate territory. A guide who cannot do this is failing at the most important clinical task.

If you ever find yourself in a healing relationship where the guide is not maintaining appropriate limits, where the relationship is becoming personal or romantic or secretive or dependent, please speak with an independent therapist. What is being offered is not healing. It is a reenactment, and it will ultimately cause harm.
A skilled guide will welcome a conversation about transference. They have been trained to work with it. If naming what you are feeling is met with discomfort, dismissal, or reciprocation, that is information about whether the guide is equipped to hold the work safely.
For Helpers Specifically
💛
When the Helper Is in the Chair
The Helpers Wound
Helpers often have a particular relationship to transference that is worth naming. Many were shaped into caretakers before they were shaped into selves, which means their earliest relational template involved earning love and safety through service. Being useful was what made them acceptable. Having needs was what made them a burden.

When a helper sits in the chair, the transference that arises is often organized around these themes. Will the guide still approve of me if I fall apart? Am I too much? Am I disappointing them? If I need too much, will they leave? The guide becomes, in the transference, the parent who required performance, or the parent who was emotionally unavailable, or the parent who was there physically but whose love always felt conditional.

Helpers also bring a particular counter-transference risk when they become guides themselves. The healer who has not done their own work on the helper wound is at risk of unconsciously organizing the healing relationship around their own need to be needed. They may resist the person in the chair becoming well because wellness ends the relationship. They may find themselves pulled toward clients who are more dependent or who offer more gratitude. These are not character flaws. They are unexamined relational patterns, and they are exactly what ongoing supervision and personal work exist to address.

Whether you are in the chair or holding space for someone else, the work of understanding your own transference and counter-transference patterns is not optional. It is the foundation of safe, ethical, effective healing work.
The helpers who do the deepest work on their own relational patterns become the most trustworthy guides. Not because they no longer have transference, but because they know it when they see it, in themselves and in the people they serve, and they have the skills and the support to work with it skillfully.
✦ A Note for Helpers
When transference shows its face in a healing relationship, it is not a sign that something has gone wrong. It is a sign that something important has arrived. The feelings are real. Their source is older and deeper than the current relationship. Following them back with curiosity, rather than shame or alarm, is where the healing lives.

You may love your guide in ways that surprise you. You may feel anger toward them that seems disproportionate. You may crave their approval in ways that remind you of something much older. All of it is welcome. All of it is information. All of it can be brought into the light.

And if you are a helper who is also a guide, the same invitation applies. Know your patterns. Do your work. Get supervision. The people who sit with you deserve a guide whose history is visible to themselves, if not yet fully healed.

Transference is not the enemy of healing. It is one of its most reliable roads.
