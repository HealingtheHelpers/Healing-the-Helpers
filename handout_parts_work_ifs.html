Parts Work and IFS

An introduction to Internal Family Systems and why getting curious about your parts changes everything

There are no bad parts. Every part of you developed for a reason, and every part deserves to be understood rather than exiled.

What Parts Work Actually Is
🧩
We Are Not a Single Self
The Model
Internal Family Systems, developed by therapist Richard Schwartz, begins with a simple and radical premise, that the human mind is not a single unified self but a system of distinct inner parts, each with its own perspective, feelings, memories, and motivations. This is not a sign of disorder. It is the normal architecture of the human psyche, and understanding it changes the entire experience of inner conflict.

You have likely felt this without having a name for it. The part of you that wants to rest and the part of you that cannot allow rest. The part that craves intimacy and the part that withdraws when it gets too close. The part that knows you are enough and the part that is convinced you are not. These are not contradictions or character flaws. They are parts, and each one has a history, a function, and something important to say.

Alongside these parts, IFS identifies a core Self, a stable, compassionate, curious presence that exists in every person and that is capable of holding and healing all the parts. The Self does not need to be built or developed. It is already there. The work of IFS is to access it and bring it into relationship with the parts that have been running the system in its absence.
The goal of IFS is not to eliminate or silence any part. It is to unburden the parts that are carrying pain they were never meant to carry permanently, and to help all parts trust the Self enough to work together rather than against each other.
The Three Types of Parts
IFS organizes parts into three broad categories. Understanding which type you are working with changes how you approach the work.
🏛️ Managers
Proactive protectors that work in advance to keep things safe. They maintain control, plan ahead, manage how you present yourself, keep emotions in check, and ensure that the vulnerabilities underneath never get exposed. The overachieving helper, the perfectionist, the one who cannot delegate or rest — these are often manager parts doing their job. They are trying to protect you by keeping everything under control.
🔥 Firefighters
Reactive protectors that activate when the exile's pain breaks through despite the managers' best efforts. They act fast and without much care for consequences. Impulsive eating, drinking, scrolling, rage, dissociation, or any behavior that quickly numbs or distracts from overwhelming pain. Firefighters are not trying to cause harm. They are trying to put out the fire of unbearable feeling as fast as possible, whatever it takes.
💔 Exiles
The youngest and most vulnerable parts, usually formed in childhood, that carry the original pain, shame, fear, or grief. Exiles hold the memories and beliefs from the wounding experiences. They believe things like "I am not enough," "I am unlovable," or "I am fundamentally broken." Managers and firefighters exist largely to keep the exiles hidden, because the exile's pain is so old and so overwhelming that the system concluded it was safer to lock it away than to feel it. Exiles want nothing more than to be seen, held, and told that what happened was not their fault.
The Self
The healer is already inside you.
The Self in IFS is characterized by what Schwartz calls the eight Cs, being curiosity, calm, compassion, confidence, courage, creativity, clarity, and connectedness. When you are in Self, you can be with any part without being overwhelmed by it, without blending with it, without needing it to be different. You can hold a firefighter's shame and an exile's grief with the same warmth you might offer a frightened child.

The Self is not built through achievement or spiritual attainment. It does not need to be earned. It is the natural state of the human psyche when the parts are not running the show. The work of accessing it is the work of parts healing, where each part is listened to, unburdened, and allowed to relax, the Self becomes more consistently available. Not a destination, a gradual returning.
Why Parts Develop
🧒
Every Part Had a Good Reason to Form
Compassion for the System
Parts do not develop randomly. They develop in response to specific experiences, usually in childhood, in which the environment required a particular adaptation. A child in a household where anger was frightening learns to suppress anger. The part that does the suppressing is not a flaw. It is a survival strategy, developed in an environment where the expression of anger was genuinely dangerous.

A child who was responsible for managing a parent's emotional state learns to be hypervigilant to others' needs and to subordinate their own. The helper wound, which so many in this work carry, is not a character flaw. It is a part that learned, in a specific relational context, that love was safer when earned through service.

Understanding parts through the lens of their origin changes the relationship to them entirely. What looked like a problem becomes a protective strategy. What felt like a flaw becomes evidence of intelligence and adaptability. The shame that often accompanies discovering one's patterns begins to lift when those patterns are understood as the very best response available to a young person in a situation they did not choose.
IFS does not pathologize any part. The most extreme and destructive behavior, when traced to its origin and its protective intention, reveals a part that was doing something understandable. The behavior is not endorsed. The part is understood. The distinction matters enormously for the healing.
Working With Parts
🤝
Curiosity, Not Combat
The Approach
The fundamental shift IFS asks for is from fighting parts to getting curious about them. Rather than trying to eliminate the inner critic, silence the anxious part, or override the one that wants to isolate, the work involves turning toward each of these with genuine curiosity. What is this part trying to do? What is it afraid will happen if it stops? What does it need me to know? What has it been carrying and for how long?

This approach works because it is the approach the parts are actually asking for. Most parts that present as obstructive, loud, or destructive are simply trying to be heard. When they are approached with curiosity and compassion rather than judgment and suppression, something shifts. They often become more cooperative, less extreme, and more willing to allow the Self to take the lead.

The protectors, managers and firefighters alike, will not step back until they trust that the Self can handle what they have been protecting. This trust is earned through relationship, the same way all trust is. By showing up consistently, by listening without flinching, by demonstrating that you can be with the exile's pain without being destroyed by it. When the protector trusts that, it can finally rest.
A useful starting practice. When you notice a strong internal reaction, pause and ask "which part of me is activated right now?" Then ask it what it is trying to do for you. The simple act of shifting from "I am anxious" to "a part of me is anxious" creates enough distance to get curious rather than consumed.
🔓
Unburdening
How Parts Heal
When a part, particularly an exile, has been sufficiently witnessed and understood by the Self, something becomes possible that was not possible before, which is unburdening. The exile can release the beliefs, emotions, and sensations it has been carrying, often since childhood, because the Self is now present enough to receive what the exile was holding alone.

Unburdening in IFS is not a technique so much as a natural outcome of genuine contact between the Self and the exile. When an exile feels truly seen and understood, when the Self says "I see you, I know what you have been carrying, and you do not have to carry it alone anymore," the exile can release what it has been holding. The release is often felt physically, as a lightening, a softening, a shift in the body.

After unburdening, the exile can take on qualities it was prevented from expressing while burdened, playfulness, curiosity, joy, creativity. And the protectors that were organized around keeping that exile hidden can relax into new roles. The firefighter who was drinking to suppress the exile's pain may discover it prefers to spend its energy differently when the exile is no longer in distress.
Parts Work in Medicine Sessions
✨
The Medicine Bypasses the Protectors
The Connection
One of the most illuminating ways to understand what happens in medicine-assisted healing is through the IFS lens. The managers and firefighters that ordinarily keep the exiles locked away cannot maintain their usual level of control in the altered state. The medicine temporarily overrides the protectors' ability to run the system, which is why material becomes available in a session that was not available in ordinary therapeutic work.

This is not always comfortable. When the protectors come down and the exiles get access to the surface, what emerges can be intense and disorienting if there is no framework for understanding what is happening. Understanding IFS before a medicine session can make the difference between a person moving through what surfaces with curiosity and a person being overwhelmed by it. Knowing that what is arriving is an exile, carrying old pain that deserves to be witnessed rather than a current crisis that needs to be managed, changes the entire relationship to the experience.

The guide's role in that window is, in IFS terms, to help hold the Self so that the Self can be present with the parts that are surfacing. When the guide maintains calm, curious, compassionate presence, they are modeling and mirroring the Self-energy that the person's own system needs to access. This is a clinical and a relational act simultaneously.
Integration after a medicine session is, in parts work terms, the ongoing conversation between Self and the parts that surfaced. There is also something physiological happening alongside the psychological. When the protectors come down and the exile's old survival response finally moves through the body and arrives at safety, a loop that has been open since the original wounding gets to close. The nervous system marks the cycle complete. The exile that was still living inside the original threat gets to experience a different outcome, and the body, not just the mind, registers that it survived. This is why the shaking, the trembling, and the physical releases that happen in a session are not to be managed away. They are the parts completing the cycle they never got to finish.
For Helpers Specifically
💛
The Helper Part and What It Is Protecting
The Helpers Wound Through an IFS Lens
Most helpers have a very active manager part that organized around caretaking early in life. This is the part that monitors others' emotional states, anticipates needs, steps in before being asked, and subordinates its own needs to maintain harmony and earn belonging. It is a manager because it works proactively, keeping potential relational threats at bay by making itself indispensable.

What this manager is protecting is almost always an exile that believes love is conditional. That belonging requires earning. That the moment the helper stops performing, they will be revealed as not enough and left. The manager works tirelessly to prevent that exile from ever having to experience that confirmation.

The healing for helpers is not to silence the helper part. It is to get curious about it, to understand what it is protecting, and to begin to develop enough trust with the exile underneath that the manager does not have to work so hard. When the exile's core belief, that love is conditional, begins to be updated through direct experience of being loved without performing, the manager can begin to relax. The helper can choose to help from genuine care rather than from the terror of not being enough.

Common protector parts in helpers also include a competence manager that keeps things looking capable at all costs, an emotional suppressor that maintains professional composure by keeping feeling at arm's length, and often a self-critic that preemptively attacks before anyone else can. Each of these, understood from the inside, reveals an exile it has been working to protect, and an intelligence that deserves to be honored even as it is asked to step back.
A helper who understands their own parts is a more effective guide. Not because they have finished their healing, but because they can recognize when their own manager is showing up in a session, when their own exile is getting activated, and when they need to return to Self before continuing to hold the container for someone else.
✦ A Note for Helpers
There are no bad parts. Not the one that wants to hide. Not the one that needs to be needed. Not the one that has been working so hard for so long to keep everything together that it has forgotten what rest feels like. Not the one carrying the grief it was never allowed to put down.

Every part of you deserves to be known. Not fixed, not silenced, not overcome. Known, with the same quality of curiosity and compassion you have brought to the parts of others for your entire career.

You are the person you have been waiting for. The Self that can hold all of it is already inside you. The work is simply the ongoing practice of finding your way back to it, and staying long enough to let the parts know you are there.
